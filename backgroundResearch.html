Background Research - Radio Astronomy and Magnetic fields

Magnetic fields are an interesting phenomenon. They are the movement of electrically charged particles in a plane, or the movement of electromagnetic force. Particles have specific electrostatic properties when they interact in specific ways with things known as virtual photons. Virtual photons are basically functions that explain fluctuations in the electromagnetic field, fluctuations that give particles their charge, based on how they interact with one another. It is well known that these particles exert force on one another, if they did not all particles would move in a straight line. A magnetic field is simply the vectors created from an electric current (i.e moving electric force, moving charges). These vectors, or the magnetic field lines, show the directions that particles would move if they were to get close to the object emitting them. All things have magnetic fields, but electrons oriented in different directions cancels out the effective magnetic field. However, when this does not happen, then a magnet is created.

All the bodies in the solar system have magnetic fields, however, some are more prevalent… and more interesting than others. Jupiter is the largest planet in our solar system. A huge gas giant, Jupiter is composed of mostly hydrogen and helium, starting out as gas in the outer atmosphere, but condensing down to highly conductive liquid hydrogen, swirling down at the planet’s center. It is this sea of electrically conductive hydrogen that gives Jupiter the strongest magnetosphere in the entire solar system, spanning miles. If we could see Jupiter’s magnetosphere from earth, it would extend larger than the moon across our sky. The nature of Jupiter’s magnetosphere creates a plasma full of charged particles: protons, electrons, and other particles that create a plasma around the planet. This plasma rotates with the planet, and the conductivity of each particle in the plasma helps strip charged particles off the surface of Jupiter’s many moons, adding them to the electric current and magnetic field made around the planet.

Radio emissions from Jupiter have helped scientists understand the rotation of the planet, because whether or not you can hear Jupiter depends on what part of it is facing you, the Jovian longitude. There are specific spots along Jupiter’s rotation that send out intense radio waves. These spots do not seem to follow cloud patterns, but rather the magnetic fields themselves. A stronger portion of the magnetic field means electrons and protons accelerate more, and release a higher frequency of electromagnetic radiation, this allows astronomers to find the strength of the magnetic field on Jupiter.

One of Jupiter’s closest moons, Io is integral for the examination of electromagnetism of Jupiter. Io is a moon caught in a larger game of tug-of-war with Jupiter’s other surrounding moons, and the intense pull of the gas giant itself. These combating forces make the moon very volcanic. Within its volcanic activity, Io will spit electrons and other particles into the plasma of Jupiter’s magnetosphere. These electrons and ions are violently accelerated along the vectors of Jupiter’s intense magnetic field. This erratic movement causes the emission of radio waves, due to the discrepancies in the fields created by so many charged particles moving around in different directions.

These radio waves, constantly propagating from the planet, can be detected from the Earth. Detection of these radio waves can further tell one about the nature of Jupiter’s magnetic field, the precise location of Jovian storms, locations in Jupiter’s orbit where Io storms are most detectable from Earth. Using Central Meridian Longitude Charts (CML) can help observers of all skill levels observe Io storms for themselves. There are areas along Jupiter’s orbit, where storms on Io are most probable, due to the nature of Jupiter’s magnetic field, as well as the orbit of the other moons, exerting a conflicting gravitational force on Io . Io is virtually an electric generator, with a sky full of lightning, producing over 400,000 volts. It virtually disrupts the magnetic field on Jupiter, further exciting electrons and other ions, changing the observable radio signal, heightening it. This lightning sky makes it easy to observe, and a great gateway into the world of radio astronomy, and a great tool to understand magnetism as a whole. However, Jupiter can have observable radio emission from non-io events, because particles are still accelerated along magnetic field lines, regardless of the presence of Io. However, Jupiter’s relationship with Io and their locations in their respective orbits play a large role in detectable radio signals.
