Tips and Tricks


There were many limitations during this project. From broken receivers, F-connectors that could not seem to stay on, and nuances in software usage that ended up causing major issues caused much stress, anxiety, and many missed opportunities. Nuances and seemingly small questions with no answers led to inhibited succcess. Thus, here is my list of tips and tricks to have a successful and foolproof setup.



Tips


1. Do not overcomplicate things. You do not need to know how a car works to drive it. The same applies to amateur radio astronomy. Sure, having general knowledge on how antennas, amplifiers, spectrograms, and receivers work, a detailed and complete understanding is not necessary. There is always someone who can help answer clarifying questions, and trying to know everything at once can way complicate something. Only using the parts of the software you need, not necessarily knowing every single button or tab, can significantly increase your rate of overall success. Make sure you have a good background, but do not overcomplicate something and have it scare you off. Understanding beam, gain, and declination can help you, but utilizing the software, an in depth understanding is not necessary for this process.


2. Conductivity testing. The manual lists several ways to tell from a chart the conductivity of a dipole antenna to a receiver, but you can make a simple conductivity tester to be sure. Mine was a metal pole with another copper wire that could be connected to a multimeter to test the conductivity of both the inside and the outside of the coaxial cable leading from each dipole to the receiver. This conductivity tester takes less than 5 minutes to make, and it integral for isolating potential disconnects somewhere in the antenna setup (be it the connections between the antenna and the phasing cable, the cable and the coupler, or into the receiver), which can significantly reduce time taken to redo these connections. This way you can be sure your setup is working.

3. Chart out dates you would like to measure in advance, depending on your setup. In the case of my setup in particular, it was far, and extremely hard to get to, with no source of power whatsoever. This made running it for 24 hour periods impossible. Using RJP to look at when Jupiter would be in a beam and the overall probability, taking into account possible interference from the sun, to find the best times with the most probability to plug in the setup. HOWEVER I will append something onto this tip, which is, if you are having a lot of trouble, like I was, it could be easier to run for long periods of time and try to sift through longer files to find what you are looking for.


4. Familiarize yourself with the nuances of the software. Make a list of steps to ensure you set it up correctly. There have been multiple instances during my project where a chart did not run because the timed observation was not set up correctly. Make a list of what you need to do every time to ensure success.


5. Setup: make sure, especially if your site is hard to get to, you have a weatherproof box to keep a computer, chargers, receiver, amplifier, etc, in. I attached mine to a big board inside the box, so they would not slide around.


6. Using crimp F-Connectors. One of the main difficulties I had in the constructing process was F connectors continuously falling off. The screw on F - Connectors that come with the kit work fine, but if you are anything like me, that is way out of your league. Using crimp on connectors makes it so much easier to adjust, and put on the F-Connectors, mainly so they do not fall off, forcing you to put them on again.

Tricks


1. Make it your own! Add something of your own to the project. For example, at the beginning I used a Raspberry Pi and an amplifier to record audio recordings, not spectrograms, so I could try to hear the sounds as sounds. It alows you to not only hear Jupiter or the Sun, and to be able to identify S and L bursts, but also do listen to sweepers [which are used by scientists to see the refelctivity of the ionosphere], which sound super cool.
